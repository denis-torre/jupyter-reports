Analysis of differential gene expression signatures induced by HIV infection of podocytes

Overview

The following notebook contains the results of a computational analysis of gene expression signatures generated by HIV infection of podocytes.

The analysis was performed on two different datasets:

  1. A dataset consisting of 21 samples of primary podocytes, isolated from different patients. Samples are divided in three groups:
    • HIV-infected (7 samples)
    • GFP-infected controls (6 samples)
    • Untreated controls (8 samples)

  2. A dataset consisting of 14 samples of podocyte-derived cell lines. Samples were processed in three batches, and are divided in two main groups:
    • Untreated controls (3 samples)
    • HIV-infected samples (11 samples), separated in the following timepoints:
      • 6 hours post-infection (3 samples)
      • 12 hours post-infection (2 samples)*
      • 24 hours post-infection (2 samples)*
      • 48 hours post-infection (3 samples)

*In order to have a minimum of 3 samples necessary to compute differential expression, the two timepoints were merged in a single 12-24 hour timepoint.

Key Findings

  • Data is significantly affected by batch effects, whereby most of the variance in the data is related to the batch or patient of origin. Computational methods partly assist in recovering the original biological variance.
  • HIV-infected podocytes in both datasets display overexpression of genes involved in the following pathways:
    • defense response and negative regulation of virus replication
    • immune response and cytokine signaling
    • TNF signaling
  • HIV-infected podocytes in both datasets display underexpression of genes associated to the Polycomb-group (PcG) family, including targets of the following genes:
    • SUZ12
    • EED
    • KDM2B
    • RNF2
  • The sterol biosynthesis and steroid hormone response pathways are commonly dysregulated in both datasets, showing both overexpression and underexpression of related terms.
  • A number of small molecules which reproducibly mimic and reverse the HIV infection signatures are identified:
    • Mimic: CGP-60474, mitoxantrone, alvocidib, and others.
    • Reverse: Emetine Dihydrochloride Hydrate, QL-XII-47, and others.

Computational Methods

Each dataset was analyzed using the following pipeline:

  1. Normalize raw expression levels using DESeq2's Variance Stabilizing Transformation method (1).
  2. Perform a Principal Components Analysis (PCA) to assess separation between samples.
  3. Remove batch effects from the data using the ComBat algorithm (2).
  4. Calculate a differential gene expression signature using the Characteristic Direction method (3).
  5. Perform an enrichment analysis of the top 500 most overexpressed and underexpressed genes using Enrichr (4)
  6. Identify small molecule perturbations which mimic or reverse the gene expression signatures identified in step 4. (5)

Results

1. PCA Analysis

In order to assess the separation between samples in an unbiased manner, a Principal Components Analysis (PCA) was performed on both datasets.

The PCA analysis on the primary podocyte dataset (Figure 1A) shows that similarly treated samples do not clearly cluster together. This might be due to the presence of batch effects, as some samples coming from the same patient cluster together despite being treated differently. The same analysis was repeated after batch effect removal by ComBat (Figure 1B). The algorithm was able to correct the dataset in order to ensure a better separation between samples which underwent similar biological treatment .

The PCA analysis on the cell line dataset (Figure 2A) shows that samples strongly cluster by batch. This batch effect overrides the variance contributed by the HIV infection treatment. The same analysis was repeated after batch effect removal by ComBat (Figure 2B). As above, the algorithm was able to correct the dataset in order to ensure a somewhat better separation between samples which underwent similar biological treatment .

2. Enrichment Analysis

In order to investigate the commonly dysregulated pathways following HIV infection podocytes, an enrichment analysis was performed on the differential gene expression signatures calculated comparing untreated samples to infected samples.

The enrichment analysis in the primary podocyte dataset reveals the following:

  • Upregulation of genes involved in pathways relative to immune response and inflammation. Enriched terms include defense response to virus, cytokine-mediated signaling pathway, and negative regulation of viral genome replication
  • Downregulation of genes involved in pathways relative to cell proliferation and cell migration. Enriched terms include regulation of kidney development, leukocyte transendothelial migration, and stress fiber
  • Dysregulation of genes involved in sterol biosynthesis and response. Upregulated genes are enriched for terms such as cholesterol biosynthetic process and abnormal lipid homeostasis, while downregulated genes are enriched for terms such as response to steroid hormone and response to estrogen.

The enrichment analysis in the cell line dataset reveals the following:

  • Upregulation of genes involved in defense response to virus, and TNF signaling at the 6 hour timepoint, reaching a peak of enrichment at the 48 hour timepoint.
  • Upregulation of genes involved in steroid binding at the 6 hour timepoint, which decreases in the latter timepoints.
  • Downregulation of genes involved in pathways relative to cell metabolism and the mitochondrion at the 6 hour timepoint, decreasing in the latter timepoints. Enriched terms include mitochondrial respiratory chain complex I, NADH dehydrogenase activity, and ubiquinone biosynthetic process
  • Downregulation of genes involved in kinase inhibition, reaching a peak at the 12 hour timepoint. Enriched terms include kinase regulator activity, and cyclin-dependent protein serine/threonine kinase regulator activity.

A comparison of enrichment results between the primary podocyte and the cell-line 48 hour HIV infection signatures reveal several commonly enriched pathways:

  • Upregulation of genes involved in defense response, negative regulation of viral genome replication, and TNF signaling.
  • Downregulation of genes associated to the Polycomb-group (PcG) family, including targets of SUZ12, EED, KDM2B, and RNF2.

3. Small Molecule Identification

Overview

In order to identify small molecules which mimic or reverse HIV infection signatures, a query was performed using L1000CDS2 with the following differential gene expression signatures:

  • The primary podocyte signature, HIV-infected vs. GFP-infected
  • The podocyte cell line signatures, HIV-infected vs. uninfected controls at the following timepoints:
    • 6 hours post-infection
    • 12-24 hours post-infection
    • 48 hours post-infection

For each of these, the top 50 most similar and most opposite signatures were identified amongst the library of small molecule perturbations from L1000. Drugs were then ranked according to how frequently they appeared within the top 50 signatures.

Results

The analysis revealed the following results:

  • CGP-60474 (link), a dual CDK1/CDK2 inhibitor, reproducibly mimics the later timepoints of cell line HIV infection (12, 24, and 48 hours), as well as the HIV infection signature in primary podocytes. The function of the compound is not well characterized.
  • Mitoxantrone (link), a topoisomerase inhibitor, is ranked within the top mimics for all the HIV infection signatures.
    • It is an FDA-approved drug used for treatment of multiple sclerosis, prostate cancer and certain types of leukemia.
    • It has been shown to inhibit viral replication and assembly in independent studies (Altmann et al., Antiviral Res. 2012, Deng et al., J Virol. 2007)
  • Emetine Dihydrochloride Hydrate (link), a natural plant alkaloid, is ranked within the top reversing drugs for all HIV infection signatures.
    • Emetine has been shown to inhibit HIV-1 replication by interfering with reverse transcriptase activity (Chaves et al., Molecules 2015).
    • It has also been shown to display antiviral activity against human Cytomegalovirus (Mukhopadhyay et al, PLoS Pathog. 2016) and Dengue virus (Low et al., J. Antivirals & Antiretrovirals 2009)
  • QL-XII-47 (link), a natural plant alkaloid, is ranked within the top reversing drugs for all HIV infection signatures.
    • Emetine has been shown to inhibit HIV-1 replication by interfering with reverse transcriptase activity (Chaves et al., Molecules 2015).
    • It has also been shown to display antiviral activity against human Cytomegalovirus (Mukhopadhyay et al, PLoS Pathog. 2016) and Dengue virus (Low et al., J. Antivirals & Antiretrovirals 2009)
